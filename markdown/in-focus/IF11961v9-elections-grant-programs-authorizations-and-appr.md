Congress first authorized major federal grant programs for elections in the [Help America Vote Act of 2002](https://crsreports.congress.gov/crs-product/R46949) ([HAVA](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209&edition=prelim); [P.L. 107-252](https://crsreports.congress.gov/bill/107th-congress/house-bill/3295/text)). HAVA was enacted in response to issues with the conduct of the 2000 elections. Like previous federal elections statutes, it set requirements for the administration of federal elections. Unlike previous elections statutes, it also provided for grant programs to help states meet those requirements and identify and implement other improvements to election administration.

No new federal elections grant programs on the scale of HAVA's have been authorized as of this writing. Grant programs have been established for certain more limited purposes, however, such as [improving the collection of election data](https://crsreports.congress.gov/110/statute/STATUTE-121/STATUTE-121-Pg1844.pdf#page=154). Congress has also appropriated further funds under HAVA's grant programs, such as [funding for FY2020](https://www.eac.gov/grants/CARES) to help states address the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic on administration of the 2020 elections. 

This In Focus provides an overview of the elections-specific grant programs Congress has established and funded to date. It also briefly discusses non-elections-specific grant programs that have been used to support elections-related activities.

Elections-Specific Grant Programs

HAVA authorized two general grant programs for states:

- a [general improvements grant program](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter1&edition=prelim) that was designed to help states improve the administration of elections; and
- a [requirements payments program](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter2/partD/subpart1&edition=prelim) that was intended primarily to help states meet the requirements set by Title III of HAVA but could also be applied to more general election administration improvements if a state limited its spending on them to a specified level or had already met the HAVA requirements.


The act also authorized more specialized grant programs aimed at [facilitating or incentivizing](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title52-section20902&num=0&edition=prelim) activities [related](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter2/partD/subpart3&edition=prelim) to [voting systems](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter2/partD/subpart4&edition=prelim), [accessibility](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter2/partD/subpart2&edition=prelim) for [voters with disabilities](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter2/partD/subpart5&edition=prelim), [youth voter participation](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter2/partD/subpart6&edition=prelim), and [poll worker recruitment](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title52/subtitle2/chapter209/subchapter5&edition=prelim).

The [Military and Overseas Voter Empowerment (MOVE) Act of 2009](https://www.eac.gov/sites/default/files/document_library/files/Military-and-Overseas-Voter-Empowerment-%E2%80%9CMOVE%E2%80%9D-Act.pdf) (enacted as [part](https://crsreports.congress.gov/111/statute/STATUTE-123/STATUTE-123-Pg2190.pdf#page=129) of [P.L. 111-84](https://crsreports.congress.gov/bill/111th-congress/house-bill/2647/text)) set new requirements for military and overseas voting and registration and authorized new funding for HAVA requirements payments to help states meet them. Federal grant programs have also been established since HAVA for [reimbursing](https://crsreports.congress.gov/108/statute/STATUTE-117/STATUTE-117-Pg11.pdf#page=444) certain costs of replacing voting systems, [improving](https://crsreports.congress.gov/110/statute/STATUTE-121/STATUTE-121-Pg1844.pdf#page=154) election data collection, and [conducting](https://crsreports.congress.gov/111/statute/STATUTE-123/STATUTE-123-Pg2190.pdf#page=145) pilot programs to test new election technologies for military and overseas voters.

HAVA's two general grant programs were not originally designed—and had not historically functioned—as regular sources of new elections funding for states. Congress has returned to one of them in recent years, however, to provide states with further funding. It appropriated $400 million under HAVA's general improvements grant program to [help](https://crsreports.congress.gov/116/plaws/publ136/PLAW-116publ136.pdf#page=250) states address elections effects of COVID-19, as well as $380 million for [FY2018](https://crsreports.congress.gov/115/plaws/publ141/PLAW-115publ141.pdf#page=215), $425 million for [FY2020](https://crsreports.congress.gov/116/plaws/publ93/PLAW-116publ93.pdf#page=145), $75 million for [FY2022](https://crsreports.congress.gov/117/plaws/publ103/PLAW-117publ103.pdf#page=220), $75 million for [FY2023](https://crsreports.congress.gov/117/plaws/publ328/PLAW-117publ328.pdf#page=221), $55 million for [FY2024](https://crsreports.congress.gov/118/plaws/publ47/PLAW-118publ47.pdf#page=91), $15 million for [FY2025](https://crsreports.congress.gov/119/plaws/publ4/PLAW-119publ4.pdf#page=18), and $45 million for [FY2026](https://crsreports.congress.gov/119/bills/hr7148/BILLS-119hr7148enr.pdf#page=295) for more general purposes.

With the exception of the COVID-19-related funding—which states had to either obligate by December 31, 2020, or return to the U.S. Treasury—funds provided under the requirements payments and general improvements grant programs have been available to recipients indefinitely. States are not required to spend funds received under those programs, or any interest the funds generate, within a particular timeframe.

Other Grant Programs

The [U.S. Election Assistance Commission](https://crsreports.congress.gov/crs-product/R45770) ([EAC](https://www.eac.gov)) is the only federal agency dedicated to helping improve the general administration of elections, but other agencies have experience or expertise that may be relevant. The relevance of other agencies' experience and expertise to elections is reflected in some of the choices Congress made about the administration of the elections-specific grant programs described in the previous section of this In Focus. For example, although the EAC oversees most elections-specific grant programs, Congress assigned responsibility for some of the disability access and military and overseas voting funding to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) and the U.S. Department of Defense (DOD), respectively.

The relevance of other agencies' work to elections is also reflected in the availability of some non-elections-specific funding for elections-related activities. A complete account of all of the federal grant programs that have been or could be used to support elections activities is beyond the scope of this In Focus, but U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) and U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS) programs offer some illustrative examples. Following reports of an increase in threats to election workers in and after the 2020 election cycle, DOJ [confirmed](https://bja.ojp.gov/JAG-funding-to-combat-threats-against-election-workers.pdf) that some of its grants could be used to address such threats. DHS has reimbursed certain elections costs of disasters, such as [Hurricane Katrina](https://www.nass.org/sites/default/files/Election%20Cybersecurity/report-NASS-emergency-preparedness-elections-apr2017.pdf#page=11); [required](https://www.fema.gov/grants/preparedness/homeland-security/fy-25-nofo) recipients of some of its homeland security grants to allocate part of the funding to election security projects; and [encouraged](https://www.fema.gov/grants/preparedness/state-local-cybersecurity-grant-program/fy-24-nofo) state and local cybersecurity grantees to include election officials on their planning committees.
